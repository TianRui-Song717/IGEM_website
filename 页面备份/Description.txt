            <!--1. BACKGROUND-->
              <div class="row general_info_element" id="general_info_ele_1">
                <h3><span>Project Background</span></h3>
                <p>
                  Lead is one of persistent toxic substances (PTS), and lead pollution is typical heavy metal pollution in soil environment. Stable lead ions in soil can accumulate through crops, and finally enter human body, causing great impact on the ecosystem and human health.
                </p>
                <img style="width: 80%; margin: 1em 0px;" src="https://2020.igem.org/wiki/images/1/11/T--NAU-CHINA--Description_1_real.png">
                <p>
                  The Institute for Health Metrics and Evaluation (IHME) estimated that, in 2017, lead exposure accounted for 1.06 million deaths and 24.4 million years of healthy life lost (disability-adjusted life years) worldwide due to long-term effects on health. The IHME also estimated that, in 2016, lead exposure accounted for 63.2% of the global burden of idiopathic developmental intellectual disability, 10.3% of the global burden of hypertensive heart disease, 5.6% of the global burden of the ischaemic heart disease and 6.2% of the global burden of stroke.
                </p>
              </div>
              <!--2. Current Situation-->
              <div class="row general_info_element" id="general_info_ele_2">
                <h3><span>Current Situation</span></h3>
                <p>
                  Up to now, <b>no proper treatment methods have been developed for moderately lead-polluted cultivated lands, which are still playing a productive role. </b> The crops produced in these lands will also pose a threat to people's health and safety.
                </p>
                <p>
                  However, considering the cultivated lands that still give full play to its production performance, current methods such as the combination of physicochemical immobilization with phytoremediation and the microbial adsorption may still have some limitations :
                </p>
                <ul class="description_info_list_box">
                  <li class="description_info_list">
                    Disturbance caused by physicochemical immobilization with foreign fixing agent to the original physicochemical property of soil environment
                  </li>
                  <li class="description_info_list">
                    Negative impacts on production efficiency brought by planting a large number of lead-enriched plants
                  </li>
                  <li class="description_info_list">
                    Low fixation efficiency due to the complicated distribution of heavy metal in soil environment
                  </li>
                  <li class="description_info_list">
                    Difficulty in ensuring the survival rate of microorganisms
                  </li>
                </ul>
                <div class="col-md-12 col-sm-12">
                  <img src="https://2020.igem.org/wiki/images/0/04/T--NAU-CHINA--Description_2.png">
                </div>
<div class="clearfix"></div>
                <p>
                  To transcend limitations imposed by current methods, our team attempted to re-consider this problem with a hope of adding a new approach to treatment of soil lead pollution.
                </p>
              </div>
              <!--3.Our Inspiration-->
              <div class="row general_info_element" id="general_info_ele_3">
                <h3><span>Our Inspiration</span></h3>
                <p>
                  Based on the current situation, we were inspired by the research on lead immobilization. Lead in soil can form <b> exceptionally stable pyromorphite [Pb<sub>5</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>X (X = Cl, F, OH)]</b> in the presence of enough soluble phosphate with a K<sub>sp</sub> of about 10<sup>-60</sup> - 10<sup>-80</sup>. It has passed the EPA standard of the United States, and it's described to be unable to extract effective lead through the TCLP method.
                </p>
                <p>
                  This research enlightened us to form an idea: transforming lead ions into such a stable mineral.
                </p>
                <img src="https://2020.igem.org/wiki/images/c/cb/T--NAU-CHINA--Description_3.png" alt="lead to Pyromorphite">
              </div>

                <div class="col-md-6 col-sm-12">
                  <p>
                    The main component of the pyromorphite crystal is phosphate PO<sub>4</sub><sup>3-</sup>, but most phosphorus in the soil is insoluble. In the meanwhile, the complex soil environment contributes to the relatively dispersed heavy metals.
                  </p>
                  <p>
                    So, in order to achieve our goal, we need to get two essentials prepared:
                  </p>
                  <ul class="description_info_list_box">
                    <li class="description_info_list"><b>a significant amount of soluble phosphate</b></li>
                    <li class="description_info_list"><b>a certain way to relatively accumulate soil lead</b></li>
                  </ul>
                  <p>
                    In this case, native phosphorus-solubilizing microorganisms in the soil and native earthworms provide us with great help.
                  </p>
                </div>
                <img id="roadmap_img" style="display: inline-block;" src="https://2020.igem.org/wiki/images/c/c7/T--NAU-CHINA--Description_7.png">
                <p style="margin-bottom: 2.2em;">
                  Soil phosphate-solubilizing microorganisms can convert insoluble phosphorus to soluble phosphate by secreting phosphatase and organic acids. However, the phosphatase does not play a good role in the neutral environment of the earthworms' intestines. <b>Finally, we find a more suitable enzyme, phytase, which has similar function with phosphatase</b>.
                </p>
                <p>
                  As an indigenous worm in the soil, earthworms have certain tolerance and enrichment ability to heavy metals. Therefore, earthworms can be ideal helpers：
                </p>
                <ul class="description_info_list_box">
                  <li class="description_info_list">
                    Earthworms can live in the soil polluted by heavy metals and accumulate heavy metal ions in their bodies.
                  </li>
                  <li class="description_info_list">
                    Earthworms' intestines can be a good culture medium for our selected engineered bacteria.
                  </li>
                  <li class="description_info_list">
                     Earthworm’s mobility can make our engineered bacteria able to tackle with a large range of soil area.
                  </li>
                </ul>
              </div>
              <!--5. OUR STRATEGY-->
              <div class="row general_info_element" id="general_info_ele_5">
                <h3><span>Our Strategy</span></h3>
                <p>
                  <b>Our project plans to employ earthworms as mobile carriers for heavy metal treatment, and utilize <i>Bacillus subtilis</i>, the dominant symbiotic bacteria in earthworms' intestines, as engineered bacteria to achieve the enrichment and immobilization process of heavy metals.</b> Earthworms can accumulate lead ions through soil intake, and we employ some regulatory parts, such as oxygen-free inducible system, toehold switch, and IPTG-inducible system in <i>Bacillus subtilis</i> to precisely regulate the behavior of our engineered bacteria. In consideration of bio-safety, we design a suicide module in engineered bacteria.
                </p>
                <p>
                  As a result, the engineered bacteria can secrete phytase in the earthworms’ intestines, therefore transforming invalid phosphorus elements into soluble phosphate in the soil eaten by earthworms. The soluble phosphate works together with lead ions and some chloride ions which originally exist in soil, thus leading to the formation of pyromorphite. Finally, the pyromorphite and some engineered bacteria are discharged along with earthworms' excrement into the external environment. At this time, the suicide module of our engineered bacteria is triggered, thus guaranteeing the biosafety of our project.
                </p>
                 <img src="
https://2020.igem.org/wiki/images/e/e8/T--NAU-CHINA--Description_6.png">
                <p>
                    This year, due to isolation caused by COVID-19, we are unable to do our wet lab experiments. We verify our concept based on literature and we employ mathematical models to predict results and feasibility.
                </p>
